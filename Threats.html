1. How fishing afftect turtle population?

Fishing can indeed be a threat to turtle populations. Here are some ways in which fishing activities can negatively impact turtle populations:

  • Getting Entangled in Nets or Lines: The result is usually death by drowning due to forced submergence, which means the turtle can’t surface for air. Ingestion of or entanglement in gear can inhibit feeding behavior which can also lead to death.


  • Ingestion of Fishing Hooks: This can severely damage the esophagus and digestive system, and removal of these large hooks is very difficult. If snared by a hook or entangled in fishing lines, which is often what happens with long lines, cutting the line does no good; the turtle is likely to either still be entangled or swim away with a dangerous fish hook still lodged in the mouth, neck or flipper. It can be particularly detrimental if the hook is stainless steel. If a stainless steel hook is used, a turtle’s body is unable to dissolve it – even over many years.


  • Accidental Capture and Discardment: When turtles are caught by accident they are often discarded because they are not the target. Turtles are either dumped alive and injured, with no hope of survival, or already deceased, due to drowning or other injury during capture.


  • Prevention - The use of turtle excluder devices (TEDs) in fishing gear, such as trawls and gillnets, is highly effective in reducing turtle bycatch. TEDs are designed to allow turtles to escape from the nets while retaining the target catch. Promoting the widespread adoption and proper use of TEDs can significantly reduce accidental capture and mortality of turtles.Also education & trainning fishermens would save turtles. Educating fishermen about the importance of turtle conservation and providing training on best practices for bycatch reduction is crucial

2. How climate change affect turtle population?

Global warming, or climate change, poses significant threats to turtle populations in several ways:

  • Rising temperatures: The gender of many turtle species is determined by the temperature at which their eggs are incubated. With rising global temperatures, there is a risk of skewed sex ratios in turtle populations. Higher temperatures can lead to a predominance of females as warmer nests produce female hatchlings. This imbalance can disrupt the reproductive dynamics and genetic diversity of turtle populations, potentially leading to population decline.


  • Sea-level rise: As global temperatures increase, glaciers and polar ice caps melt, causing sea levels to rise. Sea-level rise can negatively impact turtle nesting beaches and their surrounding habitats. Nesting beaches may erode or become submerged, reducing the available nesting area for turtles. The loss of suitable nesting sites can lead to decreased nesting success and hatching rates, potentially affecting the overall population.


  • Coral bleaching: Rising water temperatures can trigger coral bleaching events, which occur when corals expel the algae living within their tissues, causing them to turn white. Coral reefs provide crucial habitats for turtles, supporting food sources and shelter. When corals bleach and die, the loss of these habitats can reduce foraging opportunities and disrupt the intricate ecological balance upon which turtles depend.


  • Prevention - Reducing of carbon emission. Transition to cleaner and renewable energy sources, such as solar and wind power, and promote energy efficiency measures. Decreasing carbon emissions helps mitigate global warming and reduces the impacts of climate change on turtle habitats.

3. How plastic affect turtle population

Plastic pollution poses a significant threat to turtle populations in several ways:

  • Ingestion: Turtles often mistake plastic debris, such as plastic bags, balloons, and discarded fishing gear, for food. This can lead to ingestion, where turtles consume the plastic items, which can cause blockages or obstructions in their digestive systems. Ingested plastic can be detrimental to turtles' health, leading to malnutrition, internal injuries, and even death. The ingestion of plastics is a significant cause of mortality in turtles.


  • Habitat degradation: Plastic pollution can degrade turtle habitats, both on land and in the ocean. Nesting beaches can become littered with plastic waste, hindering nesting success and hatchling emergence. In marine environments, plastic debris can accumulate on coral reefs, seagrass beds, and other important feeding grounds, disrupting the natural balance of these habitats.This can affect the availability of food and shelter for turtles, impacting their survival and overall population health.


  • Chemical contamination: Plastics in the marine environment can absorb and release harmful chemicals. When turtles ingest plastic, they may also be exposed to these toxic substances, such as persistent organic pollutants (POPs) and heavy metals. These chemicals can accumulate in the turtles' tissues over time, leading to long-term health effects, including immune system dysfunction, reproductive issues, and impaired growth and development.


  • Prevention - Proper waste disposal and recycling. Establish effective waste management systems that include proper waste collection, recycling facilities, and public awareness campaigns. Encourage individuals and communities to properly dispose of plastic waste to prevent it from entering turtle habitats and marine ecosystems. Also beach and ocean cleanup initiatives. Organize and participate in beach and ocean cleanup activities. Engage communities, schools, and organizations to collect and properly dispose of plastic waste found on beaches and in coastal areas. Regular cleanups help prevent plastic debris from harming turtles and other marine life.
